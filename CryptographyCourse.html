Course Description We need to share secrets with those we trust. But how to communicate the secret if we're not face-to-face? And who should we trust? Powerful people and institutions have met their demise because of failures to communicate secrets to trusted parties. The science of cryptography is continually advancing. This leads to an unending battle between those who encrypt secret messages and those who try to intercept them and break the encryption. We will explore the history of cryptography and predict what lies ahead. Are we prepared to keep secrets in a future that includes quantum computing?

Week-by-week Course Plan (ambitious, subject to revisions)

  1. The history of cryptography from Julius Caesar through WWII and the critical lesson learned from it: Week 1 slides
    • Answer Key to Caesar Cipher puzzles
      • Shift is 23, plaintext is ALL GOOD THINGS MUST END
      • Shift is 9, plaintext is I DECIDED THAT I'D BETTER MAKE THIS PRETTY LONG. THAT WAY, PEOPLE WILL HAVE AN EASIER TIME SOLVING IT. I HOPE I MADE IT SIMPLE ENOUGH.
    • Answer Key to Monoalphabetic Substitution puzzle:
      • Substitutions (plaintext and cipher character): W --> P, P --> C, R --> F, E --> J, S --> K, A --> N, M --> P, I --> Q, H --> R, O --> U, C --> V, D --> W, T --> X, U --> Y, L --> Z
      • Resulting plaintext phrase: THE SECRET WORD HIDES A SIMPLE CLUE
  2. The quest for the elusive one-time pad leads cryptographers to AES as the standard for symmetric encryption and a new problem -- our being overwhelmed by the number secret keys we have to remember: Week 2 Slides. Links to web pages referenced in slides appear below.
  3. Diffie and Helman make a remarkable mathematical discovery about asymmetric encryption keys. Rivest, Shamir, and Adelman show how to implement it as practical computer algorithm. It will be "unbrakeable" until it isn't. Week 3 slides
  4. Who do you trust as the keeper of your secrets and the keys that unlock them? Cryptocurrency illustrates the need to blend cryptographic hashes with RSA to attain information integrity as well as secrecy. Week 4 slides
  5. Finishing material from Week 4. We'll start quantum and Shor next week. Week 5 slides
  6. A new model for computation based on two famous questions, one from math and one from physics: Does P = NP? Is there, in Einstein's words, "spooky action at a distance"? Shor's Algorithm (1994) demonstrates that RSA will no longer work in the new quantum model of computation. Week 6 slides
  7. When will we have quantum computers in practice instead of theory? What do we do with our secrets when that happens? Week 7 slides

Book List for the Cryptograpically Curious (Additional recommendations welcomed)

  • Historical
    • Enigma: The Battle for the Code by Hugh Sebag-Montefiore
    • The Code Book: The Science of Secrecy from Ancient Egypt to Quantum Cryptography by Simon Singh
    • The Woman Who Smashed Codes: A True Story of Love, Spies, and the Unlikely Heroine who Outwitted America's Enemies by Jason Fagone. (Thanks to John Leroy)

  • Fiction

    • The Quantum Spy by David Ignatius
    • Cryptonomicon by Neal Stephenson
    • The Gold Bug by Edgar Allan Poe
      • William F. Friedman, an American cryptologist who helped break Japan’s PURPLE cipher in World War II, traced his early fascination with cryptography to reading this as a child

  • Quantum Computing and the furure

    • Quantum Computing since Democritus by Scott Aaronson
