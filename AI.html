I’m sorry, Dave, I’m Afraid I Can’t Do That: What History Could Teach Us About Designing Humane AI

Course Leader: Tom Naps

Welcome to the course home page for the spring 2024 OLLI course “I’m sorry, Dave, I’m Afraid I Can’t Do That: What History Could Teach Us About Designing Humane AI”. The course will meet in Magrath Library Room 8 for seven Thursdays 12:30 - 2:00 starting March 28. Feel free to contact me (mailto:thomas.naps@gmail.com) anytime you have questions or concerns about the course.

Course description from OLLI catalog: When HAL refused Dave’s order to open the pod bay doors in Stanley Kubrick’s movie adaptation of Arthur C. Clarke’s 2001: A Space Odyssey, was it going rogue or making a rational choice among options it had? Philosophers, writers, and scientists have long speculated on thinking machines. Have we now reached an inflection point in the relationship we will have with AI going forward? What could it be capable of? What do we want it to be capable of? How can we align the potential of AI with our own weaknesses and strengths? We will explore the answers to those questions and more.

Course outline

  • Week 1 - AI, please do the right thing! Concerns lying ahead as we emerge from the AI winter. ( Powerpoint version of slides ) ( PDF version )
  • Week 2 - Moravec’s paradox. Big data meets video game hardware and AI’s trajectory changes from rule-based heuristics to deep learning. ( Powerpoint version of slides ) ( PDF version )
  • Week 3 - What can we learn from three case studies? IBM’s Watson, driverless cars, and facial recognition. ( Powerpoint version of slides ) ( PDF version )
  • Week 4 - LLMs: Neural nets on steroids. ( Powerpoint version of slides ) ( PDF version )
  • Week 5 - Framing the issues and open questions that your small groups will focus on next week. ( Powerpoint version of slides ) ( PDF version ). The issues are:
    1. Privacy
    2. Monopolization
    3. Geopolitics and national security
    4. Monetization
    5. Free speech
    6. Intellectual property
    7. Bias in training data
    8. Human work in the context of AI
    9. Deep fakes
  • Week 6 - Work in small discussion groups, chosen on the basis of interest in a common topic from Week 5. Tom will circulate, trying to provide “assistance” and offering the opportunity to run a query your group may have on Perplexity.ai
  • Week 7 - Informal group presentations using a “choose your adventure” format in which your group identifies options we have in deciding how to “control” AI on your issue and then predicting the outcome that will result when a particular option is chosen. Additional resources for deeper exploration

  • The Worlds I See: Curiosity, Exploration, and Discovery at the Dawn of AI by Fei-fei Li. Li is one of the world’s leading researchers in image recognition. This book interweaves the history of this area of AI with a memoir detailing Li’s journey from a young girl in China’s mail-centered education system to her present positions as Co-Director of Stanford’s Institute for Human-Centered Artificial Intelligence and co-founder/board chair of the AI4ALL organization, a non-profit dedicated to improving AI by increasing the diversity of those involved in it and thereby correcting the alignment problem (see next resource).
  • The Alignment Problem: Machine Learning and Human Values by Brian Christian.
  • Unmaksing AI: My Mission to Protect What Is Human in a World of Machines by Joy Buolamwini. When she was an MIT graduate student embarking on a research project in facial recognition, Buolamwini discovered that nearly all existing facial recognition systems failed miserably when it came to subjects who were, like her, female and black. That inspired her to dedicate her career to addressing issues of racial and gender bias in AI systems.
  • You Are Not a Parrot by Emily Bender. Bender is a computational linguist at U of Washington. This NYMagazine article presents a shorter layperson perspective on a classic academic paper by Bender in which she detailed differences between understanding and merely being able to mimic expression in a natural language. A Youtube video discussion of her research in this area appears here.
  • The Coming Wave: Technology, Power, and the Twenty-first Century’s Greatest Dilemma by Mustafa Suleyman. Suleyman was a co-founder of Google’s DeepMind, then started up his own company Inflection AI, and was recently hired by Microsoft (along with much of Inflection’s staff) to lead their consumer AI business. In this book, he gives his perspective on a future where we will need to make careful choices to avoid an AI dystopia.
  • The Little Learner: A Straight Line to Deep Learning by Daniel P. Friedman and Anurag Mendhekar. A good book if you’re interested in a deeper dive into the mathematics behind neural nets. Written in a uniquely conversational style but still requires a slow reading and ideally tinkering with the book’s code that you can download on your computer.
  • Your Face Belongs to Us: A Secretive Startup’s Quest to End Privacy as We Know It by Kashmir Hill. Hill is the privacy reporter for the NY Times. This book is a fascinating account of how ClearviewAI went full-speed ahead on a project that both Google and Facebook deemed too risky to continue.
  • God, Human, Animal, Machine: Technology, Metaphor, and the Search for Meaning by Meghan O’ Gieblyn. The author, raised in an evangelical family, reflects on how she has changed and what being human might mean in an age of AI.
  • Burn Book: A Tech Love Story by Kara Swisher Tech’s leading critical journalist reflect on her experience interacting with the leaders of technology, many of whom are deeply involved in how AI will factor into our future. She’s not easy on them when it comes to their motives. Earlier in March, as part of her book tour, she spoke at the Westminster Forum in Minneapolis - you can view a recording of the event here.
  • Four Battlegrounds: Power in the Age of Artificial Intelligence by Paul Scharre. A geopolitical perspective on AI.
  • Power And Prediction: The Disruptive Economics of Artificial Intelligence by Ajay Agrawal, Joshua Gans, and Avi Goldfarb If our minds are predictive machines, how well can AI predict and influence what those minds are going to predict?
  • The Battle for Your Brain: Defending the Right to Think Freely in the Age of Neurotechnology by Nita A. Farahany AI wants to learn how to “be one” with our human brains. Do we want that?
